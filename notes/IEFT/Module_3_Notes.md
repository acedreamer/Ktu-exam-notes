## 1. Perfect Competition
**[SURE SHOT | ↻ REPEATED PYQ: 2022, 2023, 2025]**

### A. What is this?
Imagine a local vegetable market where fifty different vendors are all selling the exact same type of tomatoes. No single vendor is big enough to change the price of tomatoes on their own. If one vendor tries to sell for ₹50 when everyone else is selling for ₹40, nobody will buy from them. If they sell for ₹30, they will lose money because they could have easily sold everything at ₹40. 

In this scenario, the market decides the price, and the vendor just "takes" it. This is **Perfect Competition**. It is an ideal, theoretical market structure where competition is at its absolute maximum. In the real world, it's rare, but agricultural markets and stock exchanges come very close.

> **Analogy:** Think of a grain of sand on a beach. That one grain of sand cannot change the shape of the beach or the level of the tide. It is just one among millions, completely powerless against the vast ocean (The Market). In perfect competition, a firm is that grain of sand.

### B. Exam-Ready Theory
**Definition:** Perfect competition is a market structure characterized by a large number of buyers and sellers, where all firms sell an identical product and no single firm has the power to influence the market price.

**Key Features (Mandatory for Exams):**
1.  **Very Large Number of Buyers and Sellers:** So many that no individual can affect demand or supply.
2.  **Homogeneous Product:** All products are identical (perfect substitutes). A consumer doesn't care which seller they buy from.
3.  **Free Entry and Exit:** No barriers (like patents or high costs) to joining or leaving the industry.
4.  **Perfect Knowledge:** Both buyers and sellers know everything about prices and technology.
5.  **Perfect Mobility of Factors:** Labour and Capital can move instantly to where they get the best returns.
6.  **Absence of Transportation Costs:** Assumed that all goods are produced locally (theoretical simplification).
7.  **Firm as a 'Price Taker':** The firm accepts the price determined by market forces (Demand & Supply intersection).

**Equilibrium Condition for a Firm:**
A firm in perfect competition maximizes profit when:
1.  **$MC = MR$** (Marginal Cost equals Marginal Revenue).
2.  **MC curve cuts the MR curve from below** (The slope of MC must be greater than the slope of MR at the point of intersection).
*Note: In perfect competition, $Price = AR = MR$ (A horizontal line).*

### C. Worked Examples
**Visualizing the Price Taker:**
```text
      MARKET (Industry)                     FIRM
Price ^      S (Supply)               Price ^
      |     /                               |
      |    /                                |
      |---E (Equilibrium)                   |---d = P = AR = MR
      |  / \                                | (Perfectly Elastic)
      | /   \ D (Demand)                    |
      +----------------> Qty                +----------------> Qty
```

**Case Study: Why do they only make Normal Profit in the Long Run?**
*   If firms make "Super Normal Profit" (Price > AC), new firms will enter the market because entry is free.
*   This increases market supply, shifting the supply curve right.
*   Market price falls until $Price = AC$.
*   Profit is wiped out back to "Normal Profit."

### D. How to Write in Exam
*   **Start With:** Define "Price Taker" vs "Price Maker." State that a perfectly competitive firm is a price taker.
*   **Body:** List and explain at least 5 features. You **must** draw the side-by-side graph showing how the Market 'X' (Demand/Supply) sets the horizontal price line for the individual Firm.
*   **Traps:** Don't forget the second condition of equilibrium (MC cuts MR from below). Just saying $MC=MR$ is only half-marks.
*   **Close With:** Explain that in the Long Run, firms only earn "Normal Profit."
*   **Mark Split:** 3 marks for definition/concept, 7 marks for features, 4 marks for firm equilibrium diagram.

### E. Rapid Recall & Self-Test
*   **1-Minute Recall:** Price Taker. Homogeneous products. $AR=MR=Price$ (Horizontal). Entry/Exit is free. Normal profit in long run.
*   **Flash Questions:**
    1. What is the shape of the demand curve for a competitive firm? (Horizontal/Perfectly Elastic)
    2. Who determines the price in perfect competition? (The Market/Industry)
    3. What does $AR$ stand for? (Average Revenue)

## 2. Monopoly
**[SURE SHOT | ↻ ALL PAPERS]**

### A. What is this?
The word "Monopoly" comes from Greek: *Mono* (Single) + *Polo* (Seller). Imagine if there was only one company in the entire country allowed to run trains (Indian Railways) or only one bank allowed to issue currency (RBI). If you want that service, you have no choice but to go to them. 

Because they are the only ones, they have "Monopoly Power." They are not price takers; they are **Price Makers**. They can set a high price, and while people might buy less, there's no competitor to steal their customers.

> **Analogy:** Imagine you own the only well in a vast desert. Everyone is dying of thirst. You can charge whatever you want for a cup of water. You are the king of that market. This is a Monopoly.

### B. Exam-Ready Theory
**Definition:** Monopoly is a market structure in which there is a single seller of a product which has no close substitutes.

**Key Features:**
1.  **Single Seller:** One firm constitutes the entire industry.
2.  **No Close Substitutes:** If there were substitutes, it wouldn't be a monopoly (e.g., if you could easily fly instead of taking the train, the railway monopoly weakens).
3.  **High Barriers to Entry:** This is the most important feature. Barriers can be:
    *   *Legal:* Patents, copyrights, or government licenses.
    *   *Natural:* Control of raw materials.
    *   *Economic:* Massive setup costs (Economies of scale).
4.  **Price Maker:** The firm has full control over the price.
5.  **Price Discrimination:** A monopolist can charge different prices to different customers for the same product (e.g., lower train fares for senior citizens).

**Demand Curve under Monopoly:**
The demand curve ($AR$) is **downward sloping**. If the monopolist wants to sell more, they *must* lower the price. Because $AR$ is falling, the Marginal Revenue ($MR$) curve lies below it and is twice as steep.

### C. Worked Examples
**Monopoly Profit (Super Normal Profit):**
If a monopolist's Price ($P$) is higher than its Average Cost ($AC$), it earns Super Normal Profit.
```text
Price ^
      | \ (MC)     / (AC)
      |  \        /
      |---\------/--- (Price set at AR)
      |   |\    /
      |   | \  /
      |   |  \/ (Equilibrium E where MC=MR)
      |   |  /\
      |___|_/__\____ (Cost at AC)
      |   |/    \
      |  / \     \ (AR = Demand)
      | /   \ MR  \
      +----------------> Output
```
*The shaded area between Price and Cost represents the profit.*

**Case: Dumping**
A monopolist sells their product at a high price in the home market (where they have no competition) but at a very low price in international markets to capture market share. This is called **Dumping**.

### D. How to Write in Exam
*   **Start With:** "A monopolist is a Price Maker." Define the term and give 1-2 real-world examples (RBI, Railways).
*   **Body:** List the barriers to entry. Draw the downward sloping AR and MR curves. Show the equilibrium point where $MC=MR$ and extend it up to the AR curve to find the Price.
*   **Traps:** Don't draw a horizontal demand curve. Also, remember that while a monopolist can set the price, they *cannot* control the quantity people will buy at that price (the market still follows the Law of Demand).
*   **Close With:** Mention "Price Discrimination" as a unique ability of a monopolist.
*   **Mark Split:** 3 marks for definition/features, 7 marks for equilibrium diagram and explanation, 4 marks for barriers to entry.

### E. Rapid Recall & Self-Test
*   **1-Minute Recall:** Single seller. No substitutes. Price Maker. Downward demand curve ($AR > MR$). High entry barriers.
*   **Flash Questions:**
    1. Why is the $MR$ curve below the $AR$ curve in a monopoly? (To sell more, price must be lowered).
    2. Name one legal barrier to entry. (Patents/Copyrights).
    3. Can a monopolist charge different prices to different people? (Yes, Price Discrimination).

## 3. Monopolistic Competition
**[NEW | HIGH PROBABILITY | ↻ ALL PAPERS]**

### A. What is this?
This is the market structure we see most often in our daily lives. Think of soaps (Dove, Lux, Pears) or restaurants (KFC, McDonald's, local diners). There are many sellers, and they are all selling the "same thing" (food or soap), but each product is slightly different.

Because their product is unique (brand, smell, taste), they have some control over the price. But because there are so many substitutes, if they raise the price too much, people will just switch to another brand. This is a blend of Monopoly (brand uniqueness) and Competition (many sellers).

> **Analogy:** Imagine a class of 50 students. Everyone is a "student" (the product), but each one has a different personality, style, and talent (product differentiation). You might prefer hanging out with one specific person, but if they become too annoying (high price), you have 49 other options.

### B. Exam-Ready Theory
**Definition:** Monopolistic competition is a market structure in which many firms sell products that are similar but not identical.

**Key Features:**
1.  **Large Number of Sellers:** Many small firms competing against each other.
2.  **Product Differentiation:** This is the **CORE** feature. Products are distinguished by brand, quality, packaging, or design.
3.  **Freedom of Entry and Exit:** No major barriers to starting a new brand.
4.  **Non-Price Competition:** Firms compete through heavy advertising and branding rather than just cutting prices.
5.  **Price Makers (Limited):** Since their product is unique, they can set their own price, but the availability of close substitutes limits their power.
6.  **Downward Sloping Demand Curve:** Similar to a monopoly, but much more **Elastic** (flatter) because there are many substitutes.

**Product Differentiation:**
Can be in the form of:
*   *Physical Differences:* Shape, size, color (e.g., iPhone vs Samsung).
*   *Service Differences:* Free home delivery, longer warranty.
*   *Imaginary Differences:* Created through advertising (e.g., "The soap of film stars").

### C. Worked Examples
**Equilibrium in the Short Run:**
The diagram is similar to a monopoly. The firm maximizes profit where $MC=MR$. Because of product differentiation, the firm can earn **Super Normal Profit** in the short run.

```text
Price ^
      |     / MC
      |    /
      |---/--- (Price set at AR)
      |  /|
      | / | (Equilibrium E where MC=MR)
      |/  |
      +--------------> Output
```

**Long Run Equilibrium:**
In the long run, because entry is free, new firms enter to grab the profits. This reduces the demand for each individual firm until they only earn **Normal Profit** ($Price = AC$).

### D. How to Write in Exam
*   **Start With:** Define Monopolistic Competition as a blend of Monopoly and Perfect Competition. Give examples of consumer goods (Shampoo, Toothpaste).
*   **Body:** Explain "Product Differentiation" in detail. List the features. Explain that advertising (Selling Costs) is a major part of this market.
*   **Traps:** Don't confuse the demand curve with a Monopoly. A monopolistic competition demand curve is **flatter** (more elastic) because substitutes are available.
*   **Close With:** Explain that in the Long Run, only normal profits are earned due to free entry.
*   **Mark Split:** 3 marks for definition, 7 marks for features (emphasize differentiation), 4 marks for short-run vs long-run profit explanation.

### E. Rapid Recall & Self-Test
*   **1-Minute Recall:** Many sellers. Differentiated products (Brand). Heavy advertising. Flatter demand curve. Normal profit in long run.
*   **Flash Questions:**
    1. What is the most important feature of Monopolistic Competition? (Product Differentiation)
    2. Who gave the term "Monopolistic Competition"? (Edward H. Chamberlin)
    3. Is the demand curve more elastic in a Monopoly or Monopolistic Competition? (Monopolistic Competition)

## 4. Oligopoly & Kinked Demand Curve
**[SURE SHOT | ↻ REPEATED PYQ: 2022, 2023, 2025]**

### A. What is this?
Think of the Indian smartphone market (Samsung, Apple, Xiaomi, OnePlus) or the soft drink market (Coke, Pepsi). There aren't hundreds of sellers, but there isn't just one either. There are a **"few" large firms**. 

The most interesting thing about an Oligopoly is **Interdependence**. If Pepsi drops its price, Coke *must* react immediately or they will lose all their customers. They are like chess players, always watching each other's moves. This leads to "Price Rigidity"—prices don't change very often because everyone is afraid of starting a price war.

> **Analogy:** Imagine four giant giants standing in a small room. If one giant moves his arm, the others must shift their positions to avoid being hit. Every move is calculated based on what the other three might do.

### B. Exam-Ready Theory
**Definition:** Oligopoly is a market structure dominated by a small number of large firms who are interdependent in their pricing and output policies.

**Key Features:**
1.  **Few Sellers:** Usually 2 to 10 firms control most of the market share.
2.  **Interdependence:** The most distinguishing feature. The actions of one firm affect the others.
3.  **High Barriers to Entry:** Brand loyalty, huge advertising budgets, and patents.
4.  **Indeterminate Demand Curve:** Because a firm doesn't know how its rivals will react, it can't be sure what its demand curve looks like.
5.  **Non-Price Competition:** Instead of fighting on price (which hurts everyone), they fight through advertising, quality, and after-sales service.

**The Kinked Demand Curve (Sweezy Model):**
Developed by Paul Sweezy to explain why prices stay "sticky" or "rigid" in an oligopoly.
*   **Logic:** If a firm **increases** its price, rivals will **not follow** (to steal its customers). So, demand is very **Elastic** for price increases.
*   **Logic:** If a firm **decreases** its price, rivals **will follow** (to prevent losing customers). So, demand is very **Inelastic** for price decreases.
*   **Result:** A "Kink" forms at the current price level.

### C. Worked Examples
**The Kinked Diagram:**
```text
Price ^
      |  \ (Highly Elastic - "If I raise, they won't follow")
      |   \
      |----K (Current Price P)
      |    | \
      |    |  \ (Highly Inelastic - "If I lower, they will follow")
      |    |   \
      +----+----> Quantity
           Q
```

**Collusive Oligopoly (Cartels):**
Sometimes, instead of competing, firms secretly (or openly) agree to set the same price. This is **Collusion**.
*   **Cartel:** A formal agreement (e.g., OPEC for oil).
*   **Price Leadership:** One dominant firm (the leader) sets the price, and others follow.

### D. How to Write in Exam
*   **Start With:** Define Oligopoly (Few sellers) and emphasize "Interdependence."
*   **Body:** List 5 features. For the "Kinked Demand Curve," you **must** draw the diagram with the sharp bend. Explain the two assumptions (Rivals won't follow price hikes but will follow price cuts).
*   **Traps:** Ensure the upper part of the curve is flatter (elastic) and the lower part is steeper (inelastic). Don't mix them up!
*   **Close With:** Mention "Non-Price Competition" (Advertising/Branding) as the primary battleground.
*   **Mark Split:** 3 marks for features, 7 marks for the Kinked Demand Curve explanation/diagram, 4 marks for Collusion/Cartels.

## 6. Product Pricing Methods
**[SURE SHOT | ↻ REPEATED PYQ: 2022, 2023, 2024]**

### A. What is this?
How do companies actually decide what number to put on the price tag? It's not always just "Supply and Demand." Depending on the product and the goal, they use different strategies. 

If it's a brand new iPhone, they start very high (**Skimming**). If it's a new brand of biscuit trying to take over the market, they start very low (**Penetration**). If they just want to be safe and cover costs, they add a standard margin to the cost (**Cost-Plus**).

> **Analogy:** Pricing is like dressing for an event. You might dress to impress and look expensive (Skimming), dress to blend in with everyone else (Going-Rate), or dress simply to cover yourself (Cost-Plus).

### B. Exam-Ready Theory
Firms use various pricing strategies based on market conditions:

1.  **Cost-Plus Pricing (Markup Pricing):**
    *   The simplest method. 
    *   $Price = \text{Unit Cost} + \text{Percentage Markup}$.
    *   *Example:* A shirt costs ₹500 to make. Company adds 20% margin. Price = ₹600.

2.  **Price Skimming:**
    *   Charging a very high initial price when a product is new and has no competition.
    *   "Skimming the cream" of the market (wealthy early adopters).
    *   Price is lowered later when competition enters.
    *   *Example:* Latest PlayStation or high-end GPUs.

3.  **Penetration Pricing:**
    *   Charging a very low price initially to "penetrate" the market and gain a large customer base quickly.
    *   Goal is to kill competition and gain brand loyalty.
    *   *Example:* Jio's initial free/low-cost data plans.

4.  **Going-Rate Pricing:**
    *   Setting the price according to the prevailing market rate or what competitors are charging.
    *   Common in homogeneous products (Steel, Cement).

5.  **Target Return Pricing:**
    *   Setting price based on a specific ROI (Return on Investment) target.

6.  **Predatory Pricing (Illegal in many countries):**
    *   Setting price so low (below cost) that competitors are forced out of business. Once they are gone, the firm raises prices to monopoly levels.

### C. Worked Examples
**Comparison Table: Skimming vs Penetration**
| Feature | Price Skimming | Penetration Pricing |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Initial Price** | Very High | Very Low |
| **Target Audience** | Wealthy / Early Adopters | Mass Market |
| **Product Type** | Innovative / Luxury | Standard / Every-day |
| **Objective** | High Profit Per Unit | High Market Share |

**Computational Example (Cost-Plus):**
A factory produces 10,000 units.
*   Total Variable Cost = ₹5,00,000
*   Total Fixed Cost = ₹2,00,000
*   Desired Markup = 25%
1.  Total Cost = ₹7,00,000
2.  Cost Per Unit = ₹7,00,000 / 10,000 = ₹70
3.  **Price** = ₹70 + (25% of ₹70) = ₹70 + ₹17.5 = **₹87.50**

### D. How to Write in Exam
*   **Start With:** Define "Product Pricing" as the process of determining what a company will receive in exchange for its products.
*   **Body:** List and explain at least 4 methods. You **must** provide the "Skimming vs Penetration" comparison table—it's a high-frequency short note question.
*   **Traps:** Don't just list the names; explain *when* each method is used (e.g., Skimming is for *innovative* products).
*   **Close With:** Briefly mention that pricing depends on both internal (cost) and external (market) factors.
*   **Mark Split:** 2 marks for definition, 3 marks for each major pricing method (Skimming, Penetration, Cost-plus). (Total 14).

### E. Rapid Recall & Self-Test
*   **1-Minute Recall:** Skimming = Start High. Penetration = Start Low. Cost-Plus = Cost + Margin. Going-Rate = Same as Rivals.
*   **Flash Questions:**
    1. Which pricing method is used for "Luxuries"? (Skimming)
    2. What is the formula for Cost-Plus pricing? ($Cost + Margin$)
    3. What is the main goal of Penetration pricing? (High Market Share)
*   **Practice Prompts:**

    2. Explain the various methods of product pricing with examples. (Part B - 14 Marks)
